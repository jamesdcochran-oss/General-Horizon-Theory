Big Idea (the one sentence version)

When Flow pushes past Capacity, systems create a boundary — a horizon — so things don’t fall apart.

Coffee cup brim = horizon. You pour past the brim → instant boundary (overflow).

Everyday: five quick pictures

1Traffic — When cars/hour > road capacity, the jam front is a “movement horizon.”

2Networks — Packets exceed a router’s buffer → a drop/queue boundary: info can’t get through.

3Plasma — Too much energy at an interface forms a sheath/shock that acts like a fence.

4Fog/Smoke — Light can’t propagate: a visual horizon. Your headlights hit a white wall.

5Black holes — Gravity packs so tight the escape speed hits light speed: the event horizon.

Light Math (labels you won’t hate)

Flow = how much you push per unit time (cars/hr, Mbps, liters/min, acres/day).
Capacity = what the system can safely carry (lane throughput, link bandwidth, pipe size, crew bandwidth).
Horizon = the boundary that appears when Flow ≳ Capacity.

Heuristic:  if  Flow / Capacity  ≳ 1  →  Horizon forms

Not calculus — just a ratio near 1. When the speedometer kisses the red, expect a boundary to pop up.

How to Predict Horizons (the three-step loop)

  1. Watch the ratio: put Flow/Capacity on a gauge (mark 0.9, 1.0, 1.1).
  2. Track “pushers” and “erasers”:
    • Pushers (raise Flow): wind, drought, demand spikes, high ignition pressure.
    • Erasers (reduce Capacity): fatigue, access limits, buffer fill, outages.
  3. Design soft horizons: buffers/fuses so hitting the limit bends, not breaks, the system.
Result: you get early warnings, graceful slowdowns, and fewer “oh no” moments.

Back-Radiation, explained like you’re busy

Two flavors:

ASky → Ground: the sky acts like a light blanket. Clear & dry nights leak heat (cooling). Cloudy & humid bounce heat back (less cooling).

Math vibe: Ground radiates up; sky radiates down. The difference is your net longwave — that’s what fuels feel overnight.

BFire → Fuel: flames “glow” heat onto nearby stuff. Bigger/hotter/closer flames with clear air = more radiant shove → faster pre-heating → easier ignition.

Use it: step away a few meters, add a moist barrier, or break line-of-sight and you slash radiant load.

Real-world Examples (bite-size)

Hawaii wind-driven fires

  • Pushers: extreme gusts, very low RH, cured grasses, utility faults.
  • Erasers: unsafe ops in hurricane-force gusts, comms/egress stress.
  • Triggers: pre-emptive de-energization; upwind staging; corridor hardening; multi-channel alerts.

Bald Mountain (VA, 2025)

  • Pushers: post-frontal NW gusts, low RH, receptive fine fuels, spotting terrain.
  • Erasers: steep access, narrow burnout windows, night limits.
  • Triggers: indirect + anchor/branch; lee-side closures; dozer/module pre-position.

Same playbook also fits Piedmont/Tidewater fall pattern (FFWI ≥ 50 + dry days + human ignitions).

FAQ (fast answers)

“Is this just a metaphor?”
No — the ratio predicts where boundaries form. The metaphors help you feel it first.

“Do I need equations?”
Only enough to know which way the needle moves. The dashboard matters more than the derivation.

“What do I actually do?”
Raise capacity, smooth the flow, or add buffers. Pick the cheapest lever that moves the ratio away from 1.
